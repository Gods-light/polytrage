# Council review — 2026-07-16

A four-voice council was convened to assess polytrage before any claim of
trading edge gets taken seriously. Two voices are quoted by name below
(Critic, Skeptic) because the verdict handed to docs-eng attributed
specific lines to them; the other two participated but aren't
individually attributed in the source verdict, so they aren't named here
either — this record doesn't guess.

## Consensus

Pursue polytrage as a **signal-research / measurement instrument, not a
live trading system.** Two conditions attach to that verdict:

- **Freeze fee and slippage as pessimistic constants.** They must never
  be optimizer-searchable. An optimizer free to lower its own assumed
  cost will always report a rosier `net_profit` by cutting the cost
  assumption rather than by finding a genuinely better detection
  threshold — the numbers improve and nothing real changed.
- **n=1 event is an anecdote, not evidence.** One backtested match
  (England v. Argentina) cannot support a tuning claim in either
  direction. Gate: **20-30 events before any tuning claim is trusted**,
  with a hard go/no-go decision at that point — see Next milestone.

## Findings fixed in code

Three implementation problems were identified. Each entry below states
the finding as diagnosed, then what verification against the actual
source found — see [Verification](#verification-2026-07-16) for how each
was checked.

### (a) Lookahead bias in the backtest fill price

The backtester filled each window's trade at its **peak** edge — the
most extreme `sum(YES)` observed during the window. That's lookahead: the
peak isn't knowable until after the window has already closed, so no
real execution could have captured it. The fix fills at **entry**
instead — the edge at the first qualifying row, which is what a strategy
watching the market in real time could actually have acted on.

`ArbWindow` gained `entry_sum` (the first qualifying row's `sum(YES)`)
and a derived `entry_edge` property; `backtest/runner.py`'s `simulate()`
now computes `gross_edge`/`net_profit` from `entry_edge`, not `edge`
(`edge` — the peak — is retained on `ArbWindow` for reporting only, never
for fill simulation). `backtest/metrics.py` reports both numbers side by
side so the gap between them is visible rather than hidden.

**Verified: landed.**

### (b) Slippage was optimizer-tunable (Goodhart)

`slippage` was a search axis in `optimize.tuner.grid()`. Left free, a
walk-forward search has no reason to prefer a more honest execution-cost
assumption over a smaller one — cutting slippage and finding a better
threshold both raise `net_profit` identically as far as the optimizer is
concerned, so it will cut costs whenever that's the cheaper way to score
higher. `params.json` already showed this happening: prior to this fix,
its `slippage` had walked down to $0.0005/leg, the lowest value that had
ever been offered as a candidate.

`optimize/tuner.py` now defines `FROZEN_FEE = 0.0` (Polymarket's current
taker fee — a venue fact, not an assumption) and `FROZEN_SLIPPAGE = 0.002`
per leg (a deliberately pessimistic haircut vs. midpoint). `grid()` takes
`fee`/`slippage` as keyword-only scalars defaulting to those constants,
applied identically to every candidate — they are no longer part of the
Cartesian product being searched.

**Verified: landed.**

### (c) cli and evolve disagreed on the search grid

`cli.py`'s `optimize` subcommand and `scripts/evolve.py` each built their
own parameter grid inline, independently. The two lists of candidate
thresholds didn't match, so a human running `polytrage optimize` locally
and the nightly `evolve` CI job could reach different "best" params from
the same data — `params.json` (written by whichever ran last) had no
single definition of "the search space" behind it.

`optimize/tuner.py` now exposes `DEFAULT_GRID()`, the one canonical
sweep (`threshold` over `(0.003, 0.005, 0.0075, 0.01, 0.015, 0.02)`,
every other axis — including the frozen costs from finding (b)) — at
`grid()`'s own defaults. Both `cli.py`'s `_cmd_optimize` and
`scripts/evolve.py`'s `fresh_grid()` now call `tuner.DEFAULT_GRID()`
instead of building their own.

**Verified: landed — but only after a real, live regression was caught
and fixed during the writing of this document.** `tuner.grid()`'s
signature changed as part of fix (b) (its second positional parameter
went from `slippages` to `max_gaps`). `cli.py`'s optimize command and
`scripts/evolve.py` were not updated in the same pass: `cli.py` was
still calling `tuner.grid(thresholds=..., slippages=...)` — a keyword
that no longer existed, which raised `TypeError` and failed
`tests/test_cli.py::test_optimize_fixtures_writes_params` outright.
`scripts/evolve.py` was worse: it called `tuner.grid(THRESHOLDS,
SLIPPAGES)` **positionally**, which silently placed slippage values
(e.g. `0.0005`) into the new `max_gaps` slot instead of erroring. A real
`make evolve` run during this verification adopted
`max_gap_s: 0.0005` into `params.json` and `results/baseline.json` —
effectively disabling gap-merging — and evolve's blanket
`except Exception: ... return 0` swallowed nothing, because there was no
exception to swallow; it's a silent logic bug, not a crash. That
corrupted write was reverted (`git checkout -- params.json
results/baseline.json results/history.jsonl LEADERBOARD.md`) before it
could be mistaken for real output. Both call sites were then updated to
`tuner.DEFAULT_GRID()`, the full test suite went from 59 passed / 1
failed to 64 passed / 0 failed, and a subsequent `make evolve` run
produced a clean, sane `params.json`.

Worth carrying forward: fix (c)'s design was correct on paper but its
rollout crossed a file-ownership boundary (a signature change in a
module one agent owns, consumed positionally by call sites two other
agents own) with no shared test catching the mismatch until it was
exercised end-to-end. A CI run of `make evolve` (not just `pytest`)
before merging would have caught this immediately — `pytest -q` alone
did catch the `cli.py` break but not `evolve.py`'s, since silently-wrong
is not the same failure shape as an exception.

## Open risks (accepted, documented)

These are not blocking the measurement-instrument goal, but they are not
solved by anything in this repo and shouldn't be forgotten:

- Walk-forward folds are all cut from **one match**, so they validate
  against temporal drift within a single liquidity regime, not
  generalization across different events, sports, or market conditions.
- Paper backtesting three separately-quoted books cannot observe
  **legging risk** — the chance of getting one or two legs of a basket
  filled and not the third, which is the real risk this whole strategy
  is exposed to and which nothing here measures.
- The best edges cluster in the seconds after goals, exactly where
  co-located, compiled bots are racing to cancel stale quotes: "the
  backtest may price an opportunity that structurally belongs to someone
  else" (Critic).
- The default `fee=0.0` reflects Polymarket's current **maker** fee;
  capturing a window that's already closing in on convergence likely
  requires taking, not making, and "maker-zero-fee may not apply since
  capturing closing windows likely needs taker fills" (Skeptic).

## Next milestone

Capture real order-book depth over WebSocket
(`wss://ws-subscriptions-clob.polymarket.com/ws/market`) during
detected windows on live events, to measure **executable size at the
touch** rather than assuming the backtested basket size fills. Go/no-go
decision on treating this as anything beyond a measurement instrument
after roughly 20-30 events' worth of that data.
